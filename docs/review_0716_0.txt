The proposed recovery idea is promising, but the current presentation should be tightened substantially. I would recommend keeping the density-ratio simplex formulation and using SPA as the main recovery algorithm, especially for (m\ge 3). Katz-style residue operators are useful for defining contamination/irreducibility and give a clean endpoint interpretation when (m=2), but iterative pairwise residue purification is not the right general algorithm for (m\ge 3), since it can stop at non-pure mixtures. A joint vertex-recovery formulation is the more appropriate route.

Several points need correction or clarification.

First, the paper should distinguish carefully between pairwise irreducibility, joint irreducibility, and anchored/separable identification. The current text appears to treat anchored separability as exactly equivalent to irreducibility. This is too strong in general. For vertex recovery, one needs a condition ensuring that each vertex (v_j) lies in the closure of the support of the ratio-vector distribution, equivalently that there are regions where the responsibility (\beta_j(x)) can approach one while all other responsibilities vanish. Pairwise conditions of the form (\kappa^\star(Q_j|Q_k)=0) do not automatically imply such simultaneous anchor behavior for (m\ge 3). The theorem should state the actual required separability/joint-irreducibility condition explicitly.

Second, the direction of the contamination ratios should be double-checked. If the anchor condition for component (j) is (q_k(x)/q_j(x)\to0) for (k\neq j), then the corresponding (\kappa^\star) statement has to use the matching orientation. Since (\kappa^\star(F|H)=\operatorname*{ess,inf} f/h), reversing (F,H) changes the meaning. The current exposition should be revised so that the ratio orientation matches the claimed anchor interpretation.

Third, the SPA exactness lemma should be separated into a population identification result and a finite-sample algorithmic result. At the population level, it is enough to show that the vertices (v_j) are identifiable as extreme points of (\overline{\operatorname{conv}}\operatorname{supp}{\bar r(X)}). At the finite-sample/noiseless matrix level, exact SPA recovery requires the stronger separability condition that each vertex actually appears as a column of (R). In continuous models, sample points will generally only approach the vertices, not equal them exactly. Thus the current statement “Assumption anchor, noiseless case” should not be used as if it implied exact finite-sample anchor columns. The finite-sample theorem should instead state approximate recovery under an explicit near-anchor and ratio-estimation error condition, with dependence on the conditioning of (V).

Fourth, the scaling and reconstruction steps should be made fully explicit. Once (V) is recovered, (\omega^Q) is determined by (V\omega^Q=\mathbf 1_L), provided this system has a unique positive solution. Then (\Pi=V\operatorname{diag}(\omega^Q)), and the intrinsic source ratios are recovered by
[
q_j(x)=\sum_l \alpha_{lj}\bar r_l(x),
\qquad
\alpha=(\Pi^+)^\top.
]
This is correct under full column rank of (\Pi), but the paper should verify the dimensions, uniqueness of the scaling, positivity of (\omega^Q), and row-stochasticity of the reconstructed (\Pi). If the finite-sample implementation clips negative values via a positive part, the resulting density ratios should be renormalized to integrate to one.

Finally, the empirical implementation needs more caution. Since the method relies on estimated density ratios, and SPA selects extreme points, it may be highly sensitive to classifier miscalibration, tail errors, and outliers. The paper should either use a robust/regularized SPA variant or add trimming, calibration, bootstrap stability checks, and a clear error propagation argument from (\widehat{\bar r}) to (\widehat V), then to (\widehat\Pi), and finally to (\widehat Q_j). Without such analysis, the population identification result is plausible, but the practical recovery claim is stronger than what has been justified.

In short, I would keep the density-ratio vertex formulation and SPA, use Katz residues mainly for interpretation and for the (m=2) endpoint case, and revise the theory around a precise anchored/joint-separability assumption plus a separate finite-sample stability theorem. The main idea is sound, but the equivalence between irreducibility and separability, the exact SPA claim, and the finite-sample density-ratio implementation should be checked very carefully.
